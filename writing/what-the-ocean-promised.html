What the Ocean Promised

on my mother, and the parts of her story I've had to piece together

The harsh fluorescent lighting blinds my vision as I adjust to the austerity of the hospital: beige tiles and strips of brown carpet racing down the floors; cautionary posters plastered across sickeningly blue walls; nurses with thick Spanish accents, whose words are incomprehensible to my untrained ears; the stiff, iron-ringed, white curtains that separate the doctors from their patients, who seem to wave white flags of their own as I pass by their windowless rooms. The artificial stench of hospital antiseptic burns the back of my throat. I can feel my eyes begin to well with fat teardrops. I instinctively seek the comfort of my mother's hand, her firm leather skin cracked and calloused by the years, but my fingers grasp thin air.

I stand in the stillness of the waiting room as two surgeons operate on my mother's chest. I can picture the droplets of blood glistening on their scalpels, the wet thunk of flesh echoing through the chamber, the only noise that muffles their breaths and hushed voices.

My mother was diagnosed with Stage 2 breast cancer ten years ago. She found out the results of her biopsy on the day before Thanksgiving in 2016, shortly after her forty-fifth birthday, but thankfully the doctors caught it early. They said the tumor was located slightly underneath her left breast, malignant enough to require immediate surgery, but not fatal. Six months of chemotherapy and radiation treatment would follow the surgery.

I will not describe to you the miraculous wonders of surgeons and chemotherapy, detail my mother's recovery, or explain how the medicine has changed her personality. Cancer, albeit her deadliest challenge, is but a fraction of what life has thrown at her in its unjust bouts of anger. God, fate, or whatever higher powers you may believe in, have wronged her time and time again with its cruel choices. Nevertheless, I will share with you the story of my mother's life in its entirety — one she has forgotten in the depths of her mind, or perhaps buried in the past, left behind to decay over time.

✦ ✦ ✦

My mother, born on September 9, 1972, in Shandong Province, China, to two professors at Jinan University with an older sister four years her elder, crawled out of the womb kicking and crying, rattling with the ferocity of a lioness and an iron grip on the umbilical cord that attached her to the familiar world. At least, that is how I like to imagine her birth. Sometimes it is easier for me to think that she was born like this, with her fierceness — her unrelenting desire to live — innate from the start. Then the rest of her story falls perfectly into place, and I do not have to relive the moments that made her so tough and unbreakable.

In truth, I do not know much about her childhood or early twenties. There are eight years, from her college graduation to her arrival in the United States, when she worked at her first corporate job in Shanghai, that she declines to speak about. I have a feeling it is a certain person from the past she refuses to revisit, but I never push too far beneath the surface because then she curls back into her shell. So I will tell you the bits and pieces that I know, the fragments of memories that sometimes slip out unnoticed when her mouth makes a rounded O-shape as if she has suddenly remembered a secret, forbidden to all but those who lurk in the shadows, awaiting the forgotten.

✦ ✦ ✦

My mother's story begins on the sixth floor of a squat, gray apartment complex that vaguely resembles a nuclear bomb shelter, the standard living arrangement for university employees during post-Cultural Revolution China, the awkward interval between Chairman Mao's reign and the country's gradual opening to foreign influence. Together with her sister, she slept in a small wooden bed in the center of the apartment, beside a rickety bedside table that served as both dining area and school desk. Back then the luxury of "living rooms" and "bedrooms," labels defining the purpose of a room, did not exist; her house, like the houses of everyone else surviving on the brink of poverty, was divided into two separate areas: a space for the adults, and a space for the children. There was one window with a cracked, peeling frame, and the rest of the walls, no more than four strides apart, were painted a fiery red and bore an uncanny resemblance to the color of the Chinese flag. But the inside of her apartment never felt cramped.

For a girl with a naive imagination and innocent hope for what the future offered her, who dreamed of the ocean and escaping the concrete mountains of industrial China, the specks of dirt lining the corridor were grains of sand, and the folds of her quilt rippled like water, sunlit and bathing in a mellow glow, tumbling over the ledge and into the murky harbor. Slick-haired, sun-kissed, and with eyes stinging from the frothing seawater, she saw salt drying in ribbons where the waves had lapped at her legs and left a glistening trail of crystals as she lay awake at night, four hundred miles from the ocean and listening to the thrum of overhead vents and the buzz of the bustling city in place of the soft melodies of marine life. (In 2000, at the age of 28, my mother saw the ocean for the first time.)

Back then, the world was infinite and unpredictable. She was as likely to become a common laborer, if she failed to test into a four-year university as her sister had, as she was to receive an elite education at China's top universities and become the pride of her high-achieving professor parents. Her fate rested in the hands of the gaokao, China's national university entrance exam. If she performed exceptionally well, she would be one step closer to the ocean she so desperately yearned for. But if she failed, the ocean would disappear forever, and in its place, a lifetime of manual labor and blue-collar factory jobs would remain, and she would return to the slums her father had spent thirty years escaping, disgracefully banished from the city to the outskirts of Jinan.

My mother did in fact pass the gaokao (although not with flying colors) and in August of 1990, she packed the leather suitcase her parents had bought from a secondhand store with all of her worldly possessions and departed for university in Shanghai. Her pockets were filled with postcards of promises to write, love letters from her childhood sweetheart, and a meager five hundred Chinese yuan amassed from her savings to pay for tuition and miscellaneous fees. Yet she felt no more at ease than at home in Jinan, where she at least had a family to fall back on. She was the last of six girls to arrive in Shanghai, so her bed for the next four years became the bottom bunk that reeked of cigarettes, tucked away in a mournfully dull and unsuspecting corner of the dormitory.

The rest is assembled from fragments. My mother never told me the name of the university she attended, nor what happened after she graduated, but I have pieced together bits of the story through careful speculation. She remained in Shanghai for the entirety of her time there, never returning to Jinan. After graduation, she found work as a translator for a Dutch company expanding into the Chinese market. She rose as high on the corporate ladder as a woman with no inherited wealth or prominent family background could: weekends with foreign clients at high-end restaurants, younger women reporting to her in the office with the same nervous stutter she'd once had herself. On paper, her life at twenty-six was the epitome of success, more than most would dare to dream of. But she had reached a dead end. China at that time — corroded by political radicalism and financial gatekeeping — offered her no higher rung to climb. And so, she turned her attention to America, the land she hoped would satisfy the ache inside her chest — the inner child that cried to cross the ocean of freedom — and set her free from the failures of love. (I never gathered the courage to ask whether her breakup with her ex-boyfriend was the final straw. I do not know his name, or how many years they were together.)

But America only sold her lies.

Her first ten years here, until the government approved her green card in 2008, gave her nothing but premature gray hair, dark circles, and a lifeless look that has never fully left her eyes. My father and America were disappointments she learned to live with. But China was too swift a goodbye — and I think that is the wound that never closed. If she had stayed, maybe things would have turned out differently. Maybe she would have found another man, built a quieter life. Maybe I would have grown up without the perpetual hostility that clings to the floors of our house, without the cacophony of slamming doors to bid me goodnight. Maybe my mother would have been happier.

She often tells me that I am the reason why she has not thrown her life away to return to her hometown and care for her parents, who are now in their late eighties. If I am honest, sometimes I wonder why she chose to leave her life of relative comfort behind and start anew; why she never visited my grandparents even after fifteen years in a foreign country; why she winces whenever I bring up Shanghai or anything related to that city. And even though I have been wondering for eighteen years, I still do not have a satisfactory answer — not for myself, and not for you. What I have instead is this:

A group of aunties crowd a street vendor selling fresh durian, the shrill of their haggling piercing the air. Amidst the chaos of the market, children still wearing their school-issued uniforms shriek and swerve around customers. A worker squats on the side of the street for his mid-afternoon break and opens a box of meat pastries, the scent of roasted pork wafting through the air and catching the attention of a band of stray dogs. Men shout as a delivery truck from a nearby farming village parks across the street at the supermarket entrance, scrambling to grab the first box of fresh cabbages or heave the heftiest bag of short-grained rice onto their tanned shoulders. Beyond the heavily accented Mandarin cries of migrant peddlers and judgemental side glances of pedestrians, a small child speeds by on a bicycle clearly too large for her frail body and petite stature. She rounds the corner to arrive at an opening in the wall, so easy to miss that most only see deformation in the brick, and only the hungriest can spot the red sign underneath the tarp. "Popsicles," the sign reads. "Regular: 1¢; Premium: 5¢."

The child, who looks to be about seven, rummages through her pockets for the extra five cents her father had given for a special birthday treat. Her hands emerge empty. Her heart races in fear of the consequences of losing her father's hard-earned wages — would it be a spanking, the belt, or kneeling on the cold, hard concrete in the corridor outside her house, alone to drown in her thoughts? Taking one final longing glance at the bright yellow popsicles and the sweet remedies they promise, she sighs in defeat and readies her bike for departure.

I do not know for certain that this is my mother. But I know the apartment, the five cents, the belt. She told me these things once, in the distracted way she sometimes tells me things — not as confession, not as warning, but as fact, the same way she might tell me the weather. I have held them since then like objects I am not sure what to do with, turning them over in my hands, trying to understand what it cost her to become the woman standing just out of reach in a hospital waiting room, the woman I have spent my whole life trying to know.

When the leather belt slides home, finding a familiar groove on her skin and marking her back with blistering red welts, the girl closes her eyes and imagines another future. In another place, another time, or another world, maybe life wouldn't be so bad.

I think she is still imagining it. I think she has never stopped.

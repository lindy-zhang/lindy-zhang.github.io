On Leaving and Finding Home

on Upton, nostalgia, and the strange math of missing a place you were desperate to leave

The last time my mother and I drove through Upton, and I mean really drove through our town, greedily sucking in every detail, every inch of half-forgotten land our car passed by — behind the old brick elementary school we used to think was haunted and that ancient Victorian house on Mechanic Street, whose hinges looked like they were about to fall off, its paint chipped and plastic shutters cracked — I must have been about thirteen. Freshly enrolled in a new private school two towns away, surrounded by lush rolling hills and three-story mansions boasting fresh coats of white paint, and I had been whisked away from the only place I'd ever called home. "Thank God I made it out of here," I said from the passenger seat, our car parked in front of a red stoplight as I watched an elderly woman push a double stroller across the road. She wove her grandchildren through a maze of potholes with trained familiarity. I couldn't imagine living here for the rest of my life.

Leaning closer against the window, close enough that if I'd been a dog I could have smelled the faint twinge of alcohol in her breath, I observed the woman. Wrinkles covered her forehead. Tired eyes bulged from her face, which resembled a crumpled piece of paper. Her skin sagged above her shirt, sporting a bright green logo: Nipmuc High School 50th Reunion. She must have been older than I'd thought, because she had that dead look — the sort of expression you'd notice when someone had been in one place for too long. I asked my mother if she could see her face too, but she kept her eyes on the road.

"Stop ogling around," my mother snapped. "Close that mouth of yours. You'll get wrinkles when you're my age if you keep making that face."

The light turned green, and we left the woman behind in the dust.

Before

When I was a child, my favorite holiday was the Fourth of July. Not because I particularly cared for the independence of my birth country — although I could recite the Pledge of Allegiance by heart — or enjoyed those awfully noisy firework shows, when the crowd would whoop with excitement at every glittering silver shower, but because of the annual block party. The big town party, that is — the one the PTA moms started planning months in advance.

Red, white, and blue balloons lined the cul-de-sac on Main Street, and police cars with flashing lights blocked the sidewalk from careless traffic. People from every corner of the town crowded the street for the party, lawn chairs spilling into the road as toddlers in patriotic shirts tottered on unsteady legs, clutching sparklers. Big Tim, the officer who patrolled my elementary school's campus and whom I'd decided to nickname for his hulking stature, waved at me from a distance. He wasn't wearing his uniform, so I couldn't tell if it was really him, but I figured Upton didn't have another 6'1", 300-pound bald man with a beer belly. I waved back and munched on a half-eaten hot dog.

When they played the National Anthem, everyone — including the family who'd just moved to my neighborhood from abroad and barely spoke a word of coherent English — stood in respect. I looked to my right and caught the eye of a hunchbacked old man with an Army Veteran jacket. A group of teenagers with bleach-blonde hair took off their sun-faded hats and shot me a smile as they pushed back their seats. I stood with them. Crossed my hand over my heart like them. We didn't look much like each other, but we sang in unison. And the rocket's red glare, the bombs bursting in air, gave proof to the night…

Later, when people had dispersed back to their homes after the celebration, the cul-de-sac was empty again. Fallen streamers dotted the town square, and a wayward balloon had tangled itself in a tree. My parents were off somewhere chatting with a group of Chinese moms about the usual: Kumon placement tests, restaurant recommendations, and piano. I was alone, swaying to the chilly summer breeze. Cold, but not lonely. Something about the emptiness I couldn't put my finger on — the way the trees moved, leaves blowing in the wind, the color of the sunset, and the soft chirping of crickets in the background — calmed me. I sat there, alone, observing the center of town as the sun dipped below the horizon.

To me, this is what it meant to grow up in a small town. Everyone was a familiar face, every road was a route I knew like the back of my hand, and every corner evoked a memory. Place attachment theory, research that studies the emotional bonds people develop with geographic locations, suggests that children who grow up in stable environments develop a particularly deep sense of rootedness. According to environmental psychologist Maria Lewicka, who conducted a sweeping review of forty years of place attachment research, the emotional bonds people form with their hometowns is shaped not only by physical features of the landscape but also by social ties — the people, rituals, and routines that make a place feel uniquely one's own. The Fourth of July block party was exactly that: a ritual, a thread in the fabric of Upton, binding me to it in ways I didn't yet understand. Lewicka further distinguishes between what she calls "place identity," the way a location becomes woven into one's sense of self, and mere familiarity. Growing up in Upton, I had no language for this distinction. I only knew that the town felt like an extension of me.

Epiphany

When I was ten, a realization sprung upon me. It occurred to me early one morning in the heat of the Beijing summer when the sky was still a sleepy shade of pink.

Jin Jin, it's time to fetch breakfast, my grandmother whispered as she gently shook me awake from deep sleep. Come with me. Let's buy your favorite baozi from the market.

Drowsily, I stumbled downstairs, holding her hand as we weaved through crowds of bodies and middle-aged aunties haggling with street vendors. Sunlight glinted off the windows of twenty-story buildings and traditional Chinese architecture alike, reflecting rays of blinding white light into my eyes. For a moment, I couldn't see anything. Perhaps I was only imagining things, but when my eyes opened, I felt something change. The shift was imperceptible at first. As I peered into the glass windows of a neighboring skyscraper, I saw no difference in my reflection. My eyes were the same shade of brown as they'd always been. The scar from when I'd taken a tumble on the playground in second grade still marked my left cheek. And my vision seemed fine.

But the world around me looked alive. Thrumming with life. Larger and fuller than anything I'd ever seen back home in rural Massachusetts, where the closest convenience store was a ten-minute drive from my house. I stared at the rows of metal high-rises and ancient brick edifices in the Beijing skyline, towering above the paved pedestrian pathways packed with a sea of faces. The voices and scents from street vendors mixed in the humid air — garlic, soy sauce, the smoky char of roasted meat — all so sharp, so immediate that it filled every breath I took. Bicycles and mopeds darted around corners, and children zigzagged through the crowds, their laughter woven into the city's hum. I'd never seen this many people in one place. The most I'd counted on my drives to school were two walkers, a runner, and four teenagers who'd just come home for college, tanning on a porch.

For the first time, I realized how small Upton was.

It is difficult to articulate what it means to grow up in a place and then, suddenly, see it from the outside. Sociologists and geographers who study the psychology of small towns have argued that the insularity of rural communities can create a "horizon effect" — a narrowing of imagination, in which the limits of the known world correspond too closely to the limits of the physical one. For a ten-year-old who had never left New England for more than a month at a stretch, Beijing was not merely a different city. It was proof that a different scale of life was possible. That the world was not, in fact, the size of Upton.

What Beijing showed me, I would later understand, was not just the massive scale of a buzzing metropolitan hub, but also contrast. And contrast, as psychologists of perception have long noted, is one of the primary mechanisms through which we come to see the familiar at all. In her study of how children develop spatial and cultural awareness, geographer Yi-Fu Tuan argues that the experience of an unfamiliar place does not simply add to a child's understanding of the world; it transforms their understanding of home. "To know one place fully," Tuan writes, "it may be necessary to have left it." I did not know this at ten. I only knew that when I stepped out of my grandmother's apartment building into the Beijing morning, something in my chest rearranged itself, and Upton was never quite the same size again.

After

That fall, when I returned home from Beijing, I began to notice mundane details that never stood out to me before. An empty classroom across the hallway during recess, dust particles floating around in a slant of pale sunlight. Stray weeds poking through cracked cobblestone. Wisps of cloud blocking the moon at midnight, casting eerie shadows on my bedroom floor. Faded plaster walls and dim yellow lights lining the narrow corridors of the local grocery store, where the regular customers were elderly couples in their eighties and high school students skipping class. And the yellow bus that drove by every morning at 7 AM sharp, whose gray-whiskered driver would glare at me through the windows, clouded by the morning fog, whenever I opted to take the bus to school.

Walking up to the same rickety bus I'd ridden for five years, its plastic identification number now barely legible after half a decade of wear and tear, I looked up at the driver through the misty autumn haze. As usual, he refused to crack a smile, instead curving his mouth in a downturned U. His eyes were creased at the corners, as if they were accustomed to his frequent frowns. Ignoring his expression, I trudged up the steps and found my usual seat in the farthest reaches of the bus. As I trekked towards the familiar green leather of the back row, I couldn't help but observe the same tired expression on each face I passed.

Perhaps every morning was like this, when the sky was still asleep, with beams of pink streaking across the cloudless blue expanse. I wondered if my classmates found school as monotonous as I did. Had I simply never noticed their tiredness before? Or had I always been too consumed by the scenery outside the window, gazing at the green fields, the wooded forests rushing past, to see what was happening inside the bus?

With a new set of eyes sharpened by my revelation in Beijing, I took notice of my classmates aboard the bus. A resigned silence loomed over their drained faces as they slouched back in their seats. The silence was quiet — the kind of quiet that seeps into your soul when life slows to a crawl.

As the bus rumbled forward past the town center, I caught glimpses of places I'd known since childhood: the breakfast diner with its cracked neon sign, the old parking lot where I learned to ride a bike without training wheels, and the same square that held Upton's Fourth of July block party year after year. Everything was the same as it had always been.

The bus crept on, and I began to wonder. Would I be stuck here for the rest of my life? For how long would I drive across this same road, looping through these familiar streets again and again and again? In my mind, Upton held onto a piece of everyone who dared to step foot inside its boundaries. The longer you stayed, the more it would bleed you dry. Would I become one of these people? Sentenced to serve the rest of my years in rural suburbia, to live out my greatest fear?

What I didn't understand then — or really, couldn't understand, at ten or even at thirteen — was that the tiredness I read on my classmates' faces might have been more simple than I had thought. I might have simply glanced over at them at the wrong moment, catching their expressions right as they were zoning out or hit by the early school morning fatigue. I did what the newly returned traveler almost always does: misreading and overcomplicating this quietness as a sign of stagnation and defeat. Sociologist Richard Sennett, writing about the relationship between place and identity, suggests that the desire to escape one's hometown is often less about the hometown itself and more about a developmental need to construct a self that feels chosen rather than inherited. In other words, I didn't hate Upton. I was afraid of becoming someone I hadn't decided to be.

Present

In the final week of October, the air cools like clockwork in Upton, Massachusetts. Thick winter jackets emerge from hibernation, covered by dust and cobwebs. Birds call, suns fall, and nights come earlier and earlier. Trees explode with color. Bursts of burgundy red, burnt orange, and canary yellow. Time slows to a roll. Nostalgia hits. I remember.

I keep wishing I could go back in time. I'm eighteen now, enrolled in college forty miles from Upton, and I only return home four times a year. Once for Thanksgiving, once for Christmas, once for spring break, and once for summer. I remember why I hated living here so much, how trapped I felt whenever I drove through the center of town — but still I miss it. Life was easier when Upton was my biggest problem. Now I have college, friendships, and jobs to think about. Upton is the least of my worries.

It's a strange, bittersweet kind of nostalgia. Historians like Patrick Hutton, writing in the European Journal of Cultural and Political Sociology, note that the word actually started as a medical diagnosis for soldiers who were homesick for their mountains. It's an emotion that fundamentally requires distance because you can't miss home until you've left it. You cannot miss what you still inhabit. Only in leaving Upton — first for Beijing, then for boarding school, and now for college — have I been able to feel what it meant to grow up there.

Psychologist Constantine Sedikides, whose research focuses on the social and emotional functions of nostalgia, has found that nostalgic reflection tends to increase feelings of social connectedness and self-continuity. This helps explain something I've noticed in myself on those trips home: I don't return to Upton looking for the town as it is. I return looking for evidence that I am still the person who grew up there. The cracked neon sign on the diner, the parking lot where I learned to ride a bike: these are not just landmarks to me. They are, in some sense, my proof of life. From playing alone in the empty cul-de-sac across the road to now, the college student driving back through the streets with the car windows drawn down, this town, Upton, represents everything I was, everything I am, and everything I'll become.

The old woman with the stroller, the bus driver with his permanent frown, the neighbors who knew everyone's name — they were not, as I once believed, cautionary tales. They were people who had found something worth staying for. Perhaps what I mistook for stagnation was something else entirely: a choice, quiet and settled, to belong.

I wish I could tell my ten-year-old self these things and spare her from her crippling fear of small-town life. But now I'm back, in that same old town, my hometown, where I spent the first twelve years of my life, and everything comes rushing back. A flash of light, and the memories begin to play. Then I forget, for just a moment, that I ever left.
